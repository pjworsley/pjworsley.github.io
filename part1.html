Clone-By-Clone sequencing is a traditional method of sequencing large amounts of DNA. It was the primary sequencing technique used to complete the Human Genome Project.

The Human Genome Project began in 1990, with the aim to sequence the whole of the human genome. It was an international, government sponsored project, and remains the worlds largest ever collaborative bioscience project.

The Human Genome itself is over 3 billion base pairs in length, and technology at the time meant that only 300-600 base pairs could be read at a time by a method known as Sanger Sequencing. As a result of these technological limitations, various techniques had to be employed to break up the 3 billion base pairs into smaller, readable chunks.

The 'Clone-by-Clone' technique was favoured by the Human Genome Project, and was run in three main stages: Library Building, Map Building & Sequencing and Gap Filling.

Using these steps the Human Genome Project was able to divide work between multiple research labs around the world. They published their first draft of the Human Genome in 2001 which covered about 83% of the genome. It was officially completed in 2003.

The Human Genome Project was not alone in publishing their draft of the Human Genome. In 1998 a private company, Celera Genomics, began working on sequencing the Human Genome independently. By this point a large portion of the genome had already been sequenced and was publically available; because of this Celera was able to privately fund sequencing at just a fraction of the cost of the Human Genome Project.

While Celera succeeded in sequencing the Human Genome in a much shorter time, their sudden entry into the field led to much controversy, particularly against Celera's founder, Craig Venter. This was mainly due to Celera's aim to seek patent protection over many of the genes they sequenced. However, in 2000 it was announced by the US government that the genome sequence could not be patented, despite Venter's best efforts.
