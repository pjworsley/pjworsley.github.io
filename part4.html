Part 4: Shotgun Sequencing

BAC

HINT

A BAC is a Bacterial Artificial Chromosome. This BAC contains the DNA fragment.

BACs are ~150,000bp in length which is too large to sanger sequence. Shotgun sequencing means we are going to fragment this BAC into smaller pieces so they can be sequenced.

Fragmentation

HINT

The process of fragmenting a BAC. Ideally the BAC is fragmented completely randomly and each fragment should be just the right size. Also important is leaving undamaged ends which enable downstream processing.

The main methods used were physical methods (e.g. sonication) and enzymatic methods (e.g. restriction enzymes). While physical methods are much more random than enzymatic methods they can leave damaged ends to fragments. Enzymatic methods only cut at specific locations within DNA, meaning areas of the genome could be missed out, however they leave sticky ends which make the following steps easier to process.

BAC Fragments

HINT

Fragments of BAC, which is essentially the DNA broken up into very smaller pieces.

Size Selection

HINT

Size selection allows fragments of a desired size to be selected for. One selection method is electrophoresis which separates DNA fragments by mass.

This process is done to ensure no fragment was too big or to small for the DNA vectors which each fragment is place in downstream.

Fragment

HINT

A small fragments of Human DNA which has been size selected for.

This fragments of DNA is small enough to sequence a significant amount of it, meaning gaps in the DNA map can be filled in.

Sanger Sequence

HINT

Sequence the fragment of DNA using sanger sequencing.

These sequences are uploaded onto a computer.

Computate

HINT

Shotgun sequencing uses similar techniques to those seen in Part 3 in order to piece back together the sequenced fragments; crucial again are overlaps.

Because smaller fragments are used, there are many 1000 more combinations of fragments to be pieced back together, as a result greater computational power is required to piece back together shotgun sequences.

Store Fragment in New Vector

HINT

Each smaller fragment was cloned into a different vector that accepts smaller inserts. A variety of vectors were used to clone the smaller fragments because some sequences were not stable in some vectors.

These vectors were commonly stored an phage, where they could be maintained.

While Clone-by-Clone sequencing did have advantages for the Human Genome Project, it proved impossible to complete the project using this method exclusively - mostly because the middle of the BAC inserts were unable to be sequenced using sanger sequencing. To overcome this shotgun sequencing was used to fill in the gaps.

At the start of the human genome project, shot gun sequencing was still in it infancy, however by its conclusion shot gun technology proved to be far more effective than Clone-by-clone. This advancement is primarily due to the increase in computational power over the decade.

Shotgun sequencing follows much the same strategy as clone-by-clone sequencing, just with much smaller fragments of about 5,000bp as opposed to 150,000. The process is as follows:

This process was completed for each smaller fragment within an individual BAC. Because these smaller fragments were generated randomly, millions of sequences could be generated from one BAC, with multiple overlapping fragments covering similar areas of the BAC.

Sequencing these millions of smaller fragments was only made possible by the development of automated sequencing machines by Applied Biosystems.

Once sequenced, all these millions of overlapping small fragments could be fed into a computer which aligned the millions of sequences and created a consensus sequence.

Because sanger sequencing is not totally perfect and includes errors a single read can not be used to generate an accurate consensus sequence. Using both clone-by-clone and shotgun sequencing, enough end sequences were generated so that each base in the human genome had been sequenced at least 10 times from end sequences from different vectors. This created a highly accurate consensus sequence with only 0.1% errors.

While the Human Genome Project only used shotgun sequencing as a secondary technique, it was used by Celera to perform Whole Genome Shotgun Sequencing; involving fragmentation of the whole genome at once before sequencing and placing back together.

This technique required significantly more computational power than the Clone-by-clone approach, however was quicker and cheaper. The application of Whole Genome Shotgun sequencing was key to Celera ability to keep up with the Human Genome Project, however their data relied on many of the maps and sequences already published by the Human Genome Project when they started. Drafts of both the human genome project's and Celera's genome were published in 2001. Since then, sequencing has come a long way with even more computational power and increases in both accuracy and read length. However, if it has not been for clone-by-clone techniques, we may never have reached where we are today.
